Digitizing Intimacy

A Computational and Network-Based Modelling of the T.S. Eliot–Emily Hale Correspondence (1930–1969)

Overview Timeline Analysis Network Tools & Techniques Budget Eliot–Hale Letters

Overview

This project reconceptualises the Eliot–Hale correspondence (1930–1969) as a dynamic computational archive. Drawing inspiration from the Republic of Letters digital humanities initiative, it treats the letters not merely as biographical documents but as a relational network structured across time. By combining corpus linguistics, topic modelling, network analysis, and temporal visualisation, the project seeks to reveal patterns of emotional intensity, devotional language, and intellectual affiliation that unfold across four decades.

Chronology (1930–1969)

Computational Analysis

Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is applied to identify thematic clusters such as religious reflection, marital conflict, emotional dependence, artistic labour, and spiritual guilt. Sentiment modelling and lexical frequency tracking enable longitudinal measurement of tonal fluctuation across decades.

Relational Network

This force-directed network models co-occurrence relationships among key individuals, institutions, and intellectual influences referenced within the correspondence. Node centrality reflects relational prominence; edge connections indicate thematic proximity.

Tools & Techniques

The project integrates Voyant Tools for exploratory corpus analysis, enabling term frequency tracking, collocation detection, and keyword trend visualisation. Named Entity Recognition (NER) is applied to extract references to persons, institutions, religious figures, and places. These entities are disambiguated and structured as nodes within relational network models constructed using Gephi and D3.js.

Topic modelling is conducted through probabilistic LDA frameworks, allowing the identification of thematic clusters whose prevalence can be plotted chronologically. Temporal structuring is supported through TimelineJS-style modelling, aligning textual shifts with major biographical and literary milestones, including Eliot’s 1927 Anglican conversion and the composition of Four Quartets. All datasets are cleaned, structured, and version-controlled to ensure reproducibility and methodological transparency.

Budget Framework

Funding requirements are modest due to reliance on open-source tools. Core costs include research assistance for corpus cleaning and metadata tagging; server hosting and domain maintenance; technical development for advanced interactive visualisation; and dissemination through conferences and peer-reviewed publication.

Institutional support will provide infrastructure, IT consultation, and long-term digital preservation. Sustainability planning includes regular backups, repository version control, and clear documentation of scripts and data schemas. The long-term goal is to establish a durable digital research environment that contributes both to Eliot scholarship and to computational literary studies more broadly.

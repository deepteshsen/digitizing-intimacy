Digitizing Intimacy

A Computational and Network-Based Modelling of the T.S. Eliot–Emily Hale Correspondence (1930–1969)

Overview Timeline Analysis Network Tools & Techniques Budget

Overview

The Eliot–Hale letters (1930–1969) provide an unparalleled archive of emotional, theological, and intellectual exchange. This project reimagines the corpus as a structured computational environment, drawing inspiration from Mapping the Republic of Letters and applying corpus linguistics, topic modelling, and relational network analysis to examine long-term shifts in intimacy, devotion, and artistic identity.

Interactive Timeline

This chronological interface traces shifts in tone and thematic emphasis across four decades, mapping patterns of religious language, emotional vocabulary, and relational focus alongside major historical and literary milestones.

Computational Analysis

Topic modelling reveals recurring thematic clusters including religion, guilt, marital strain, devotion, and artistic labour. Sentiment analysis indicates intensification of theological vocabulary in the 1940s and relative emotional moderation in later decades.

Tools and Techniques

The project integrates Voyant Tools for exploratory lexical analysis, Gephi for network modelling and centrality metrics, TimelineJS for chronological visualisation, and Omeka as the primary archival platform. Named Entity Recognition (NER) identifies persons, institutions, and places; co-occurrence modelling constructs weighted relational graphs; and Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) identifies thematic clusters across decades. All scripts and metadata schemas are documented to ensure reproducibility and transparency.

Budget Framework

Projected funding supports research assistance for corpus cleaning and metadata tagging, server hosting and domain maintenance, technical consultation for advanced visualisation development, and dissemination through conference presentations and peer-reviewed publication. The project primarily utilises open-source tools, ensuring cost efficiency while maintaining methodological rigour. Long-term sustainability will rely on institutional hosting support and version-controlled repositories.
